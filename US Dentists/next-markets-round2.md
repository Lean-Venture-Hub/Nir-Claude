## Top 5 — Rationale

### #1 — Tampa / St. Pete, FL

- **Practices:** ~1,500 across Hillsborough (899 dentists) + Pinellas + Pasco counties
- **Population:** 3.0M metro; 1.07% growth (Tampa) + spillover from Orlando's 2.7% surge
- **Income:** ~$65K median HH; lower but retiree population is self-pay heavy for cosmetic/implant work
- **Competition:** Very Low. National agencies (Dentainment, Med Rank Interactive) serve FL but no dominant Tampa dental marketing player. Florida has 8,100+ FDA members and 3,787 residents per practice — above national average — meaning demand is high and practices need to compete
- **Why:** Florida is the fastest-growing state (23.3M population, 2024). Tampa is a major hub of retiree in-migration. Retirees are high-value dental patients (implants, full-arch restoration, cosmetic). The practice density is underserved relative to population growth. Operationally, it is a natural geographic pair with any Orlando expansion later.

---

### #2 — Nashville, TN

- **Practices:** ~900 in metro (719 specialists listed; broader metro practice count ~900-1,000)
- **Population:** 2.1M metro; 1.37% annual growth (2024 Census); added 24,000+ jobs in tech/healthcare in 2023
- **Income:** ~$72K median; rising fast due to corporate relocations
- **Competition:** Virtually None. No identifiable dominant dental marketing agency in Nashville. National agencies (Dentainment) operate there but no local specialist with market presence.
- **Why:** Nashville is the healthcare industry's corporate capital — HCA Healthcare, Envision, Quorum Health are all HQ'd there. This creates a culture where healthcare practices invest in professional management and marketing. The city is also a major DSO expansion hub, which forces independent dentists to compete. Tech worker in-migration = young, high-income patients who book online and expect digital experiences. Very low competition for a market of this sophistication.

---

### #3 — Austin, TX

- **Practices:** ~1,100 (Travis + Williamson + Hays counties; metro grew 2.06% in 2024 to 2.27M people)
- **Population:** 2.3M metro; 2.3% growth rate — one of the top 10 fastest-growing large metros in the US
- **Income:** ~$83K median HH — the highest of any Texas metro, driven by tech sector (Apple, Tesla, Samsung, Meta campuses)
- **Competition:** Low. Allegiant Digital and a few local agencies exist but no dominant dental-specific player
- **Why:** Austin is the premium version of the Texas market already being worked. Tech workers expect digital-first service discovery. New practices are opening constantly in Cedar Park, Round Rock, Leander — suburbs with young families who need a dentist from day one. The income level supports cosmetic and elective dental spend at the highest rate of any Texas market. Operationally, it leverages existing Texas infrastructure from Houston/DFW campaigns.

---

### #4 — Denver, CO

- **Practices:** ~1,200 (Colorado has ~3,600 dentists statewide; Denver-Aurora metro holds ~33% of state population)
- **Population:** 2.9M metro; ~0.9% growth — slower than Sun Belt but consistent
- **Income:** ~$94K metro-area median HH income ($108K in Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro) — second highest on this list
- **Competition:** Low-Medium. KickStart Dental Marketing (Denver-based, exclusive model) exists; national agencies serve Denver. No dominant non-exclusive dental marketing firm.
- **Why:** Denver's income level makes it the highest-value patient market on this list. Cosmetic and elective dental procedures are common among the affluent, health-conscious outdoor lifestyle demographic. A practice in Cherry Creek or LoDo can command premium pricing if marketed correctly. The income story overrides the lower growth rate — fewer new patients entering the market, but higher spend per patient.

---

### #5 — Las Vegas, NV

- **Practices:** ~900 (Clark County; Vegas is Nevada's dominant dental market)
- **Population:** 2.95M metro; 1.86% growth (2024); city growing at 1.13% annually
- **Income:** ~$60K median — lowest on the top 5 list, but self-pay dynamics compensate
- **Competition:** Very Low. Mediverticals, Lesser Media, 7Digit serve Las Vegas but none are dominant specialists. Market is notably underserved.
- **Why:** Las Vegas has a uniquely high proportion of hospitality and service industry workers — many are uninsured, making them self-pay patients highly motivated to price-shop and review-check online. The tourist-driven economy also means dental emergencies and cosmetic dental demand (veneers, whitening) are above average. Low competition despite the city's size makes this an easy entry point. Nevada has no state income tax, which supports disposable income for self-pay dental despite lower median income.

| **Los Angeles / San Diego** | Highest competition in the country; CA regulatory friction; saturated dental marketing ecosystem |
| **Miami** | 1,850+ practices but high agency competition; bilingual complexity increases production cost; lower income than other FL metros |
| **Seattle** | High income ($105K) but very saturated agencies; growth slower than Sun Belt targets |
| **Washington DC** | High income ($115K) but strong existing competition; government-adjacent culture is harder to convert |
| **San Jose/Bay Area** | Income is extraordinary but CA market is locked up; near-zero population growth |
| **Minneapolis** | Solid market but slow growth and Midwest seasonality reduces cosmetic dental spend window |
| **Raleigh-Durham** | Excellent long-term target (2.1% growth, $80K income, very low competition) but TAM (~700 practices) too small for near-term priority vs this list |
| **San Antonio** | Already covered by Texas infrastructure; lower income than Austin/DFW; growth has slowed |
| **Portland** | Flat growth + high operational cost + market not well-aligned with dental marketing spend culture |
| **Indianapolis / Columbus** | Lower income, slower growth; mid-tier markets better suited to year 3+ expansion |

## Operational Sequencing Recommendation

Given existing infrastructure in TX and FL pipelines:

1. **Austin** — run NPI + Google Maps now; leverages TX pipeline already built for Houston/DFW
2. **Tampa** — run in parallel; leverages FL regional knowledge; pairs naturally with Orlando later
3. **Nashville** — third; standalone market, very low competition, quick wins likely
4. **Denver** — fourth; build the CO playbook; premium income market worth the new-state setup
5. **Las Vegas** — fifth; very easy market entry due to near-zero competition; good ROI on low investment
