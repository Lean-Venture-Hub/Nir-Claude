The Complete Oil Change Guide: When, Why & What Type

By Mike Thompson • February 20, 2026 • 5 min read

If there's one single thing you can do to extend the life of your vehicle, it's this: change your oil on time, every time. Oil is the lifeblood of your engine. It lubricates dozens of moving metal parts spinning thousands of times per minute, absorbs heat, captures contaminants, and prevents the kind of metal-on-metal friction that turns a healthy engine into an expensive paperweight.

Yet oil changes remain one of the most misunderstood maintenance items. How often do you really need one? Is synthetic oil worth the extra cost? What happens if you go too long? Let's clear up the confusion with straightforward answers based on twenty years of turning wrenches.

How Often Do You Really Need an Oil Change?

The old "every 3,000 miles" rule was great advice in 1985. Modern engines, improved oil formulations, and better filtration systems have changed the game significantly. Here's what actually determines your oil change interval:

  • Your owner's manual — This is always your starting point. The engineers who designed your engine know it best. Most modern vehicles specify 5,000-7,500 miles for conventional oil or 7,500-10,000 miles for full synthetic.
  • Your driving conditions — If you do a lot of short trips under 10 miles, frequently idle in traffic, drive in extreme heat (hello, Austin summers), tow heavy loads, or drive on dusty roads, you're operating under "severe" conditions and should change oil at the shorter end of the recommended interval.
  • Your oil type — Full synthetic oils genuinely last longer than conventional oils. They resist breakdown, handle temperature extremes better, and maintain their protective properties longer.
  • Your vehicle's oil life monitor — Many newer vehicles have an intelligent oil life monitoring system that factors in engine temperature, driving conditions, and mileage to tell you when it's time. These are generally reliable and worth following.
💡 Mike's Honest Take

For most Austin drivers using full synthetic oil in a modern vehicle, I recommend every 6,000-7,000 miles or every 6 months, whichever comes first. It's a conservative interval that provides excellent protection without being wasteful. If you're mostly highway driving with a newer car, you can safely stretch to 8,000-10,000 miles with synthetic.

don't overthink it!

For Austin's extreme summer heat and the stop-and-go traffic many of us endure, synthetic oil is almost always the smarter choice.

Important: If your owner's manual specifies synthetic oil, you must use it. Many modern engines, especially turbocharged and direct-injection engines, are designed to operate with synthetic oil's superior thermal and lubrication properties. Using conventional oil in these engines can cause accelerated wear and potentially void your warranty.

What Happens When You Skip Oil Changes?

We've seen it too many times: someone goes 15,000 or 20,000 miles without an oil change because life got busy. Here's the progression of what happens inside your engine:

First, the oil breaks down. As oil ages, its additive package depletes. The detergents that keep your engine clean stop working. The anti-wear additives thin out. The viscosity modifiers that help the oil flow properly at different temperatures degrade.

Then, sludge forms. Broken-down oil, combined with moisture, fuel contamination, and combustion byproducts, creates a thick, tar-like sludge that coats internal engine surfaces. This sludge restricts oil flow through narrow passages, starving critical components of lubrication.

Finally, damage accelerates. Metal components begin wearing against each other without proper lubrication. Bearings score. Cam lobes wear. Oil passages clog. By this point, you're looking at engine repairs that can cost $3,000-$8,000 — or a complete engine replacement that can exceed $10,000.

A $75 oil change every six months versus a $6,000 engine rebuild. That's not a tough math problem. The cheapest repair is always the one you prevent.

What About Hybrid and Electric Vehicles?

If you drive a hybrid, your vehicle still has a combustion engine that needs oil changes — but the intervals can be different. Because hybrid engines cycle on and off frequently and often run at lower temperatures, the oil can actually accumulate more moisture and fuel contamination. Some hybrid manufacturers recommend shorter intervals despite the engine running fewer total hours.

For plug-in hybrids that operate primarily on electric power, the engine oil can sit unused for extended periods. While the oil doesn't wear out from use in this case, it does degrade over time from moisture absorption and oxidation. We recommend changing it at least once a year regardless of mileage.

Fully electric vehicles like Teslas don't have engine oil at all — but they do have other fluids (coolant, brake fluid, gear reduction fluid) that need periodic attention. If you drive an EV, we're happy to help with those services too.

5 Signs Your Oil Needs Changing Now

  1. Dark, gritty oil on the dipstick — Fresh oil is amber and translucent. If yours looks like chocolate pudding, it's past due.
  2. Engine sounds louder than usual — Old oil provides less cushioning between moving parts, making the engine sound rougher and louder, especially at startup.
  3. Oil change or maintenance light is on — Your vehicle is literally telling you. Listen to it.
  4. Oil level dropping between changes — While some oil consumption is normal, excessive oil use combined with dirty oil suggests it's time for a change and possibly further inspection.
  5. You can't remember your last oil change — If you have to think about it, it's been too long. Come in and we'll get you back on schedule.
