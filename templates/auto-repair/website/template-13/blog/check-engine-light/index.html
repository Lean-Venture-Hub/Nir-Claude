Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

By Mike Thompson • March 5, 2026 • 5 min read

You're driving to work on a perfectly normal Tuesday morning when that little amber light on your dashboard blinks to life: the check engine light. Your heart sinks. Your mind races. How much is this going to cost? Can I still drive? Is my car about to break down on I-35?

Take a deep breath. While you should never ignore a check engine light, it doesn't always mean catastrophe. In fact, the most common causes are surprisingly straightforward and affordable to fix. Let's walk through the five most frequent culprits we see at our Austin shop.

💡 Quick Tip

A steady check engine light means something needs attention soon. A flashing check engine light means pull over safely and call us immediately — it usually indicates an engine misfire that can damage your catalytic converter.

1. Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

This is the number one cause we see, and it's also the easiest to fix. Your gas cap creates a seal in the fuel system that maintains proper pressure and prevents fuel vapors from escaping. When that seal is broken — whether the cap is loose, cracked, or missing entirely — your car's onboard computer detects the pressure change and triggers the check engine light.

What to do: Pull over safely, remove your gas cap, inspect it for cracks or damage, then reinstall it with a firm twist until you hear it click. Drive for a day or two — if the cap was the issue, the light should turn off on its own. If the cap looks damaged, a replacement costs about $15-$25 at any auto parts store.

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your vehicle has between two and four oxygen sensors that measure how much unburned oxygen is in your exhaust system. This data helps your engine computer adjust the air-fuel mixture for optimal performance and emissions. When an O2 sensor starts failing, it sends inaccurate readings that trigger the check engine light.

A bad oxygen sensor won't leave you stranded, but ignoring it has real consequences. You'll see a 10-40% decrease in fuel economy, which means you're spending more at the pump every single week. Over time, a failing O2 sensor can also damage your catalytic converter — turning a $200-$400 repair into a $1,500+ replacement.

What to do: Schedule a diagnostic appointment. We'll identify which sensor is failing and replace it. This is a straightforward repair that typically takes about an hour.

3. Catalytic Converter Issues

The catalytic converter is a critical emissions component that converts harmful gases (carbon monoxide, hydrocarbons, nitrogen oxides) into less harmful emissions. When it starts failing, your check engine light will come on, often accompanied by reduced engine performance, poor fuel economy, and sometimes a sulfur or rotten egg smell.

Catalytic converter problems are often caused by neglecting other maintenance issues. That failing oxygen sensor we just discussed? If you drive on it for months, the excess unburned fuel can overheat and damage the catalytic converter. Worn spark plugs, coolant leaks, and oil burning issues can all contribute too.

At Precision Auto Care, we always look for the root cause before replacing a catalytic converter. Swapping the converter without fixing what damaged it in the first place means you'll be back in the same situation within months.

4. Spark Plugs and Ignition Coils

Spark plugs create the tiny electrical spark that ignites the air-fuel mixture in each cylinder. Ignition coils provide the voltage the spark plugs need to do their job. When either component starts failing, you'll notice rough idling, misfires, hesitation during acceleration, and of course, that check engine light.

Modern spark plugs last much longer than they used to — typically 60,000 to 100,000 miles for iridium or platinum plugs. But Austin's summer heat and stop-and-go traffic can shorten that lifespan. If your vehicle has over 80,000 miles and the original spark plugs, they're likely due for replacement regardless of whether the light is on.

What to do: Don't ignore misfires. Each misfire sends unburned fuel into the exhaust system, which can damage the catalytic converter. A spark plug replacement is one of the most cost-effective maintenance items you can do.

5. Mass Airflow Sensor Failure

The mass airflow (MAF) sensor measures the amount of air entering the engine, which determines how much fuel the engine computer should inject. A dirty or failing MAF sensor sends incorrect air volume readings, leading to rough running, stalling, poor fuel economy, and excessive emissions.

Here in Austin, our dusty Texas conditions can clog the MAF sensor faster than in other climates, especially if you drive on unpaved roads or if your air filter hasn't been changed recently. In many cases, a simple cleaning with specialized MAF sensor cleaner can solve the problem entirely.

✅ Prevention Tip

Replace your engine air filter every 15,000-20,000 miles (or more often in dusty conditions). A clean air filter protects the MAF sensor and helps your engine breathe efficiently. It's a $20 part that can prevent hundreds of dollars in repairs.

What Should You Do When the Light Comes On?

First, don't panic. Second, pay attention to how your car is driving. If it feels normal and the light is steady (not flashing), you can safely drive to our shop for a diagnostic. If the light is flashing or you notice unusual sounds, vibrations, smoke, or loss of power, pull over safely and give us a call at (555) 847-2390.

When you bring your vehicle to Precision Auto Care, we connect our professional-grade diagnostic scanner to read the trouble codes stored in your vehicle's computer. But here's the thing most shops won't tell you: the code alone doesn't tell the whole story. A code like P0420 (catalytic converter efficiency) could be caused by the converter itself, a bad O2 sensor, an exhaust leak, or even bad spark plugs. That's why we always perform a thorough diagnosis before recommending repairs.

We photograph our findings, send them to your phone, and explain everything in plain English before we do any work. You'll know exactly what's wrong, what it costs, and why it matters — and you'll never be pressured into a repair you don't need.
