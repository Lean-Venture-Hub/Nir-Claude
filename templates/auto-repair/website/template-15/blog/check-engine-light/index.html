Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

By Precision Auto Care March 5, 2026 4 min read

That little amber light on your dashboard just flickered to life, and your stomach dropped. We get it. The check engine light is one of the most anxiety-inducing warnings in any vehicle, mainly because it could mean almost anything — from a loose gas cap to a failing catalytic converter. The good news? Most of the time, the cause is straightforward and affordable to fix.

At Precision Auto Care, we run diagnostics on check engine lights every single day. After nearly two decades in business, we've identified clear patterns. Here are the five most common reasons that light comes on, what each one means for your wallet, and when you need to act immediately.

1. Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

This is the most common cause and the easiest fix. Your fuel system is a sealed, pressurized environment. When the gas cap is loose, cracked, or missing entirely, the system detects an evaporative emission leak and triggers the check engine light.

What to do: Pull over safely and check your gas cap. Tighten it until you hear it click. If the cap looks cracked or the rubber seal is worn, replace it — they cost $10-$20 at any auto parts store. The light should reset on its own after a few drive cycles, though it can take a day or two.

Cost to fix: Free (tightening) to $20 (new cap).

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your car has between two and four oxygen sensors that measure how much unburned oxygen is in your exhaust. This data helps your engine control module adjust the fuel-to-air ratio for optimal performance and emissions. When an O2 sensor fails, your engine runs less efficiently — you'll notice worse gas mileage before you notice anything else.

Why it matters: A bad oxygen sensor won't leave you stranded, but ignoring it can reduce fuel economy by up to 40% and eventually damage your catalytic converter, which is a far more expensive repair. Most vehicles can safely drive to a shop within a week, but don't put it off longer than that.

Cost to fix: $150-$300 including parts and labor.

3. Catalytic Converter Issues

The catalytic converter converts harmful carbon monoxide into carbon dioxide. When it fails, you'll typically notice reduced acceleration, a sulfur or rotten egg smell, and dark exhaust smoke. This is often the result of a neglected upstream problem — like that failing O2 sensor we just discussed, or worn spark plugs that cause unburned fuel to reach the converter.

"In 90% of catalytic converter failures we see, the root cause was something else that went unaddressed for too long. Regular maintenance prevents expensive cascading failures." — Mike Thompson, Owner

Cost to fix: $800-$2,500 depending on the vehicle. This is why catching smaller issues early saves serious money down the line.

4. Mass Airflow Sensor (MAF) Failure

The MAF sensor measures the amount of air entering your engine, which determines how much fuel to inject. When it malfunctions, you'll experience rough idling, stalling, difficulty starting, or a noticeable drop in power during acceleration. Your gas mileage will suffer too.

What causes it: Most MAF failures come from a dirty or contaminated sensor. Aftermarket air filters that are over-oiled, or simply years of dust and debris buildup, coat the delicate sensor wire. Sometimes a good cleaning with specialized MAF cleaner solves the problem entirely.

Cost to fix: $20 for MAF cleaner (DIY), or $200-$400 for sensor replacement with labor.

5. Worn Spark Plugs or Ignition Coils

Spark plugs ignite the fuel-air mixture in your engine's cylinders. Over time, they wear out and misfire — creating a rough idle, reduced power, poor fuel economy, and that dreaded check engine light. Modern iridium spark plugs last 80,000-100,000 miles, but conventional plugs may need replacement at 30,000 miles.

When spark plugs are severely worn, the ignition coils work harder to compensate, which can cause them to fail too. Replacing both at the same time is often the smart move.

Cost to fix: $100-$250 for spark plugs, $150-$300 per ignition coil.

When to Worry — Red Flags

Most check engine lights indicate issues you can address within a few days. However, pull over and call a tow truck immediately if you notice:

  • Flashing check engine light — this means active misfires that can damage your catalytic converter in minutes
  • Loss of power combined with unusual sounds — could indicate serious internal engine damage
  • Overheating alongside the check engine light — continuing to drive risks a blown head gasket
  • Strong burning smell or visible smoke — pull over immediately for safety
