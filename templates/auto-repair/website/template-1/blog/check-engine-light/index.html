Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

By Mike Thompson March 10, 2026 5 min read

You're driving along and suddenly that little amber light pops up on your dashboard. Your heart sinks. What does it mean? Is your car about to break down? Should you pull over immediately? Take a deep breath. While the check engine light should never be ignored, it doesn't always signal a catastrophic problem. In fact, many of the most common causes are relatively straightforward and affordable to fix.

At Precision Auto Care, we diagnose check engine lights every day. Here are the five most common reasons that light turns on, what each one means for your vehicle, and what you should do about it.

1. Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

Believe it or not, a loose gas cap is one of the most frequent triggers for the check engine light. Your vehicle's fuel system is sealed to maintain proper pressure and prevent fuel vapors from escaping into the atmosphere. When the gas cap is loose, cracked, or missing entirely, the system detects a leak in the evaporative emission system (EVAP) and triggers the warning light.

What to do: Pull over when it's safe and check your gas cap. Make sure it clicks into place when you tighten it. If the cap looks cracked or worn, replace it — they typically cost between $10 and $25. After tightening or replacing the cap, the light may take a few drive cycles to turn off on its own. If it persists after a day or two, bring it in for a scan.

2. Oxygen (O2) Sensor Failure

Your vehicle has between two and four oxygen sensors that measure the amount of unburned oxygen in the exhaust system. This data helps your engine computer adjust the fuel-to-air ratio for optimal performance and emissions. When an O2 sensor fails or reads incorrectly, your engine can't operate efficiently.

A faulty O2 sensor won't cause your engine to stop running, but it can reduce fuel economy by up to 40% and increase emissions significantly. Over time, it can also damage your catalytic converter, which is a much more expensive repair.

What to do: Schedule a diagnostic appointment. O2 sensor replacement typically costs between $150 and $300, depending on the sensor location and your vehicle. This is a repair you don't want to put off, as the downstream effects get progressively more expensive.

3. Catalytic Converter Issues

The catalytic converter is a critical part of your exhaust system that converts harmful carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons into less harmful carbon dioxide and water vapor. When it starts failing, you may notice reduced engine performance, decreased fuel economy, and your vehicle may run at a higher temperature than normal.

Catalytic converter problems are often caused by neglecting other issues — like a failing O2 sensor or worn spark plugs — that cause the converter to overheat and deteriorate prematurely. This is one of the more expensive check engine light repairs, with replacement costs ranging from $1,000 to $2,500.

Pro Tip

Regular maintenance is the best way to protect your catalytic converter. Keeping up with spark plug replacements, O2 sensor repairs, and using quality fuel can extend its life well beyond 100,000 miles.

4. Mass Airflow Sensor (MAF) Problems

The mass airflow sensor measures the amount of air entering the engine and determines how much fuel to inject for the right air-fuel mixture. When the MAF sensor fails or becomes contaminated with dirt and oil, it sends incorrect readings to the engine computer. This can cause rough idling, difficulty starting, sudden changes in throttle pedal position, and decreased fuel economy.

What to do: Sometimes a MAF sensor just needs cleaning with a specialized MAF cleaner spray, which costs around $10. If cleaning doesn't resolve the issue, replacement typically runs between $200 and $400. Our technicians can diagnose whether cleaning or replacement is the right call.

5. Worn or Fouled Spark Plugs

Spark plugs ignite the air-fuel mixture in your engine's cylinders. Over time, they wear down, accumulate deposits, or can crack. When spark plugs are worn, you'll notice your engine misfiring, rough idle, poor acceleration, and reduced fuel economy. Modern iridium and platinum spark plugs can last 60,000 to 100,000 miles, but they do eventually need replacement.

What to do: If your vehicle has over 80,000 miles and the original spark plugs, there's a good chance they're due for replacement. Spark plug replacement typically costs between $100 and $250 for most vehicles. We also inspect the ignition coils during a spark plug service, as they can fail around the same mileage.

When Should You Pull Over Immediately?

Most check engine light situations are not emergencies. However, if the light is flashing rather than staying steady, that indicates a severe misfire that could damage your catalytic converter. In that case, reduce your speed, avoid heavy acceleration, and get to a shop as soon as possible.

If the check engine light comes on alongside a temperature warning, oil pressure light, or you notice smoke, unusual smells, or loss of power, pull over safely and call for a tow.

Don't Ignore It

Even a steady check engine light shouldn't be ignored for long. What starts as a $150 O2 sensor repair can turn into a $2,000+ catalytic converter replacement if left unchecked.
