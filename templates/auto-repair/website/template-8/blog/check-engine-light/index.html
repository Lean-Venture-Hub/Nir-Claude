Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

By Mike Thompson March 5, 2026 5 min read

You're driving along, everything feels normal, and then it appears: that small amber light on your dashboard shaped like an engine. Your stomach drops. Is your car about to break down? Is this going to cost thousands?

Take a breath. The check engine light (formally called the malfunction indicator lamp, or MIL) is your car's way of flagging a problem detected by the onboard diagnostics system. Sometimes it signals something urgent. More often, it points to an issue that's important but not immediately dangerous. The key is understanding what might be happening — and getting it diagnosed before a minor issue turns into a major repair bill.

Here at Precision Auto Care, we run check engine diagnostics every single day. After 17 years of scanning codes and tracing problems, these are the five causes we see most often in Austin.

1. Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

This is the most common — and cheapest — reason your check engine light comes on. Your fuel system is sealed, and the evaporative emission control system (EVAP) monitors that seal. If your gas cap is loose, cracked, or the rubber gasket has deteriorated, the system detects a leak and triggers the light.

What to do: Pull over when it's safe, remove your gas cap, check the rubber seal for cracks, and retighten it until you hear a click. Drive for a day or two — the light often resets on its own. If it doesn't, the cap itself may need replacing (usually under $15) or there could be a deeper EVAP issue.

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your car has between two and four oxygen sensors (O2 sensors) that measure how much unburned oxygen is in your exhaust. This data helps your engine computer adjust the fuel mixture for optimal combustion. When a sensor fails or reads incorrectly, it triggers a diagnostic code.

A failing O2 sensor won't leave you stranded, but ignoring it can reduce your fuel economy by 10-40% and eventually damage your catalytic converter — a repair that costs ten times more than the sensor itself.

Pro tip: If your gas mileage has dropped noticeably and the check engine light is on, an oxygen sensor is a strong suspect. Replacement typically runs $150-$350 including labor.

3. Catalytic Converter Problems

The catalytic converter turns harmful carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons into less harmful emissions. When it fails, you'll often notice reduced power, a sulfur or rotten-egg smell, and poor fuel economy alongside the check engine light.

Catalytic converters rarely fail on their own. Usually, they're damaged by a downstream problem — like a failing O2 sensor, bad spark plugs, or a rich fuel mixture — that's been ignored for too long. That's why addressing check engine lights early saves you money in the long run.

Cost reality: Catalytic converter replacement ranges from $1,000 to $2,500 depending on your vehicle. But the upstream problem that caused the failure might have cost under $300 to fix months earlier.

4. Mass Airflow Sensor Failure

The mass airflow (MAF) sensor measures the volume and density of air entering your engine. Your engine computer uses this data to calculate the correct amount of fuel to inject. When the MAF sensor fails or gets contaminated with dirt and oil, the engine runs poorly.

Symptoms beyond the check engine light include rough idling, stalling shortly after starting, difficulty accelerating, and decreased fuel economy. In many cases, the sensor just needs cleaning — not full replacement. A technician can use specialized MAF cleaner to restore proper function for a fraction of the replacement cost.

What to do: Don't try to clean the MAF sensor yourself unless you know exactly what you're doing. It's a delicate component and the wrong solvent will destroy it. A professional diagnosis and cleaning typically costs $80-$150.

5. Worn Spark Plugs or Ignition Coils

Spark plugs ignite the air-fuel mixture in each cylinder. Over time, they wear out — the gap widens, deposits form, and the spark weakens. Modern iridium plugs last 60,000-100,000 miles, but conventional plugs need replacing every 30,000 miles. Ignition coils, which deliver voltage to the spark plugs, can also fail and produce similar symptoms.

Signs include engine misfires (a noticeable stumble or shake), rough idle, poor acceleration, and increased fuel consumption. Left unaddressed, misfiring cylinders dump unburned fuel into the exhaust, which — you guessed it — damages the catalytic converter.

Typical cost: Spark plug replacement runs $100-$300 depending on your engine. Ignition coil replacement is $150-$400 per coil. Both are straightforward repairs that prevent much costlier problems.

When to Pull Over Immediately

A steady check engine light means "schedule a diagnosis soon." A flashing check engine light means "pull over now." A flashing light indicates an active misfire that can cause immediate catalytic converter damage. Reduce speed, avoid heavy acceleration, and get to a shop as quickly as safely possible.

Also pull over if the check engine light is accompanied by unusual sounds, smoke, steam, a strong burning smell, or a sudden loss of power. These combinations can signal serious problems that worsen rapidly with continued driving.

The Bottom Line

The check engine light isn't the enemy — it's an early warning system designed to save you money. The five causes above account for roughly 80% of the check engine lights we diagnose at Precision Auto Care. Most are affordable fixes when caught early. The expensive repairs almost always come from ignoring the light for weeks or months.

Modern OBD-II diagnostics can narrow the problem to a specific code in minutes. From there, an experienced technician interprets the code, verifies the diagnosis with hands-on testing, and gives you a clear estimate before any work begins. That's how we've done it for 17 years.
