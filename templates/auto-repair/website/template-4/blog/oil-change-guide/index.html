How Often Should You Really Change Your Oil?

March 10, 2026 · By Mike Thompson · 6 min read

If you grew up hearing "change your oil every 3,000 miles," you're not alone. For decades, that was the standard advice. But modern engines, improved oil formulations, and better filtration systems have made that rule obsolete for most vehicles on the road today.

So how often should you really change your oil? The answer depends on three factors: the type of oil you use, your driving conditions, and your vehicle's age and make. Let's break it down.

The Real Oil Change Intervals

Here's what we recommend at Precision Auto Care based on 17 years of servicing vehicles in Austin:

Oil Type Normal Driving Severe Conditions
Conventional 5,000 – 7,500 miles 3,000 – 5,000 miles
Synthetic Blend 7,500 – 10,000 miles 5,000 – 7,500 miles
Full Synthetic 10,000 – 15,000 miles 7,500 – 10,000 miles

These intervals are general guidelines. Always check your owner's manual for your vehicle's specific recommendation, as some manufacturers have different specifications.

What Counts as "Severe" Driving Conditions?

You might think severe driving means off-road trails and extreme weather, but many everyday habits qualify. In fact, most Austin drivers fall into the "severe" category without realizing it:

  • Stop-and-go traffic — Regular commuting through Austin traffic counts as severe use
  • Short trips under 10 miles — The engine never fully reaches operating temperature
  • Extreme temperatures — Austin summers routinely exceed 100°F, which puts extra stress on oil
  • Towing or hauling — Carrying heavy loads or pulling a trailer
  • Dusty conditions — Construction zones and rural roads introduce more contaminants
  • Frequent idling — Sitting in traffic or drive-throughs for extended periods
Austin Drivers

If you commute through Austin traffic in the summer heat, you're driving in severe conditions even if it doesn't feel like it. We recommend the shorter interval for most of our local customers.

Conventional vs. Synthetic: Which Should You Use?

Conventional Oil

The most affordable option. Conventional oil works fine for older vehicles with simple engine designs and low-mileage drivers. It breaks down faster under heat and stress, which is why it needs to be changed more frequently.

Synthetic Blend

A mix of conventional and synthetic oils that offers better protection than conventional at a moderate price. It's a good middle ground for drivers who want improved performance without the full synthetic price tag. We recommend this for most daily drivers in Austin.

Full Synthetic

Engineered for maximum protection and longevity. Full synthetic oil resists breakdown at high temperatures, flows better in cold weather, and keeps your engine cleaner. Most newer vehicles (2010 and up) are designed to run on synthetic oil, and many manufacturers require it.

Save Long-Term

While full synthetic costs more per change ($65–$85 vs. $35–$50 for conventional), you change it less often. Over the life of your vehicle, the total cost is similar — and your engine gets better protection.

Signs You Need an Oil Change Now

Regardless of mileage, watch for these warning signs that your oil needs changing:

  • Dark, gritty oil on the dipstick — Fresh oil is amber and translucent; dark, thick oil has lost its effectiveness
  • Oil change light or check engine light — Many modern vehicles have oil life monitoring systems
  • Engine noise — Knocking or louder-than-usual engine sound can indicate insufficient lubrication
  • Oil smell inside the cabin — This could indicate a leak or overheating
  • Exhaust smoke — Excessive blue or gray smoke may mean oil is burning in the combustion chamber

What Happens If You Skip Oil Changes?

Oil is your engine's lifeblood. Without regular changes, old oil breaks down into a thick sludge that can't properly lubricate moving parts. Here's the progression:

  1. Increased friction — Components wear faster, reducing engine life
  2. Overheating — Degraded oil can't absorb heat effectively
  3. Sludge buildup — Passages become clogged, starving parts of lubrication
  4. Engine failure — In extreme cases, complete engine failure requiring a $3,000–$7,000 replacement

A $50–$85 oil change every 5,000–10,000 miles is the cheapest insurance you can buy for your engine.
