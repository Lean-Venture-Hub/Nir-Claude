Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

March 10, 2026 · By Mike Thompson · 5 min read

You're driving along and that little amber light appears on your dashboard. Your stomach drops. Is it something serious? Is your engine about to fail? Take a deep breath. While you should never ignore the check engine light, it doesn't always mean a catastrophic problem.

At Precision Auto Care, we diagnose check engine lights every single day. In our 17 years of experience in Austin, we've found that the vast majority of cases come down to five common causes. Here's what they are and what you should do about each one.

1. Loose or Faulty Gas Cap

This is the most common — and easiest to fix — cause of a check engine light. Your gas cap seals the fuel system and helps maintain pressure in the fuel tank. When it's loose, cracked, or missing entirely, fuel vapors can leak out, which triggers the light.

Before you panic, pull over safely and check your gas cap. Tighten it until you hear a click. If the cap looks cracked or worn, replace it — they cost under $15 at most auto parts stores. The light may take a few drive cycles to reset on its own, or we can reset it quickly at the shop.

Pro Tip

After filling up at the gas station, always turn the cap until it clicks. A loose gas cap after refueling is one of the most common reasons people come in for a check engine light.

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your vehicle's oxygen sensor measures how much unburned oxygen is in the exhaust system. This data helps your engine control module adjust the air-to-fuel ratio for optimal performance. Most modern vehicles have two to four oxygen sensors.

When an O2 sensor fails, your engine may run rich (too much fuel) or lean (too little fuel). This can decrease fuel economy by up to 40% and increase emissions. Left unaddressed, a bad O2 sensor can eventually damage your catalytic converter — a much more expensive repair.

Typical replacement cost: $150–$350 depending on the sensor location and your vehicle make.

3. Catalytic Converter Problems

The catalytic converter is part of your exhaust system that converts harmful carbon monoxide into carbon dioxide. It's a critical emissions component, and when it fails, you'll likely notice reduced acceleration, decreased fuel economy, and your engine may run at a higher temperature.

Catalytic converter failure is often caused by neglecting other issues, like a faulty oxygen sensor or worn spark plugs. That's why addressing smaller problems early can save you from this much larger repair. Replacement costs typically range from $1,000 to $2,500 including labor.

Save Money

Regular maintenance — especially timely oil changes, spark plug replacement, and addressing minor issues early — is the best way to protect your catalytic converter from premature failure.

4. Bad Spark Plugs or Ignition Coils

Spark plugs ignite the air-fuel mixture in your engine's cylinders. Over time, they wear out and can misfire, causing rough idling, poor acceleration, increased fuel consumption, and of course, a check engine light.

Most modern spark plugs last 60,000 to 100,000 miles, but that varies by vehicle. If you notice your engine sputtering or hesitating during acceleration, worn spark plugs are a likely culprit. Ignition coils, which deliver voltage to the spark plugs, can also fail and trigger the same symptoms.

Spark plug replacement is one of the more affordable engine repairs, typically running $80–$200 for a full set. Ignition coil replacement may cost $150–$300 per coil.

5. Mass Air Flow Sensor Failure

The mass air flow (MAF) sensor measures the amount of air entering the engine, which is critical for determining the right amount of fuel to inject. When it fails, your engine may stall, idle roughly, or have trouble starting. You'll also notice a significant drop in fuel economy.

A dirty or clogged air filter is a common cause of MAF sensor problems. Replacing your air filter according to your vehicle's maintenance schedule can help prevent this issue. MAF sensor replacement typically costs $200–$400.

When to Act Immediately

While most check engine light triggers aren't emergencies, there are situations where you should pull over and call for assistance:

  • Flashing check engine light — This indicates a severe misfire that could damage your catalytic converter. Reduce speed and get to a shop as soon as possible.
  • Accompanied by other warning lights — If your temperature gauge is in the red or your oil pressure light is on, stop driving immediately.
  • Noticeable performance issues — Significant power loss, strange noises, or smoke from the exhaust means you should stop and have the vehicle towed.
