Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

By Mike Thompson • March 5, 2026 • 5 min read

You're driving to work on a perfectly normal Tuesday morning when that little amber light on your dashboard blinks to life: the check engine light. Your heart sinks. Your mind races. How much is this going to cost? Can I still drive? Is my car about to leave me stranded on I-35 during rush hour?

Take a deep breath. While you should never ignore a check engine light, it doesn't always mean catastrophe. In fact, the most common causes are surprisingly straightforward and affordable to fix. At Imports & American Auto Services, we diagnose check engine lights every single day — and we've found that understanding the most likely culprits goes a long way toward calming the anxiety. Let's walk through the five most frequent causes we see at our Phoenix shop.

Quick Tip

A steady check engine light means something needs attention soon — you can usually drive safely to a shop. A flashing check engine light means pull over safely and call us immediately. A flashing light typically indicates an engine misfire that can damage your catalytic converter within minutes.

1. Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

This is the number one cause we see, and it's also the easiest and cheapest to fix. Your gas cap creates a seal in the fuel system that maintains proper pressure and prevents fuel vapors from escaping into the atmosphere. When that seal is broken — whether the cap is loose, cracked, or missing entirely — your car's onboard computer (the ECU) detects the pressure change and triggers the check engine light.

The evaporative emission (EVAP) system is sensitive. Even a single missing click on the gas cap can set off the warning. And if you recently got gas, there's a good chance this is exactly what happened.

What to do: Pull over safely, remove your gas cap, inspect it for visible cracks or a worn rubber gasket, then reinstall it with a firm twist until you hear it click. Drive for a day or two and the light should reset on its own. If the cap looks damaged, a replacement runs about $15-$25 at any auto parts store. If the light persists after replacing the cap, bring it in — there may be a leak elsewhere in the EVAP system.

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your vehicle has between two and four oxygen (O2) sensors that measure how much unburned oxygen is in the exhaust. This data feeds directly to the engine control unit, which uses it to adjust the air-fuel mixture in real time for optimal performance and emissions compliance. When an O2 sensor starts failing, it sends inaccurate readings that throw off the entire fuel management system.

A bad oxygen sensor won't leave you stranded on the side of the road, but ignoring it has real, measurable consequences. You'll see a 10-40% decrease in fuel economy, which means you're spending significantly more at the pump every single week. Over time, a failing O2 sensor can also cause excess unburned fuel to enter the catalytic converter, overheating and damaging it — turning a $200-$400 sensor replacement into a $1,500+ catalytic converter job.

What to do: Schedule a diagnostic appointment. We'll connect our professional-grade scanner, identify which of the O2 sensors is failing, and replace it. This is a straightforward repair that typically takes about an hour, and your fuel economy should improve almost immediately afterward.

3. Catalytic Converter Issues

The catalytic converter is a critical emissions component that converts harmful exhaust gases — carbon monoxide, hydrocarbons, and nitrogen oxides — into less harmful emissions like carbon dioxide and water vapor. When it starts failing, your check engine light will come on (usually with code P0420), often accompanied by reduced engine performance, decreased fuel economy, and sometimes a noticeable sulfur or rotten-egg smell from the tailpipe.

Here's something most shops won't tell you: catalytic converter problems are almost always caused by neglecting other maintenance issues first. That failing oxygen sensor we just discussed? If you drive on it for months, the excess unburned fuel can overheat the converter's internal honeycomb structure, causing permanent damage. Worn spark plugs, engine coolant leaks, and oil burning problems can all contribute too.

At Imports & American Auto Services, we always look for the root cause before recommending a catalytic converter replacement. Swapping the converter without fixing what damaged it in the first place means you'll be right back in the same situation within months — and we won't let that happen.

4. Spark Plugs and Ignition Coils

Spark plugs create the tiny electrical spark that ignites the compressed air-fuel mixture in each engine cylinder, thousands of times per minute. Ignition coils provide the high voltage the spark plugs need to fire properly. When either component starts degrading, you'll notice rough idling, engine misfires (that jerking feeling during acceleration), hesitation when you press the gas, and reduced power overall.

Modern spark plugs are engineered to last much longer than the plugs your parents replaced every tune-up. Iridium and platinum plugs are rated for 60,000 to 100,000 miles. But Phoenix's brutal summer heat and our famous stop-and-go traffic on MoPac and I-35 create extra thermal stress that can shorten that lifespan significantly. If your vehicle has over 80,000 miles and the original spark plugs, they're very likely due for replacement — whether the light is on or not.

What to do: Don't ignore misfires. Each misfire event sends a dose of unburned fuel into the exhaust system, where it can overheat and damage the catalytic converter. Spark plug replacement is one of the most cost-effective maintenance items you can do — relatively inexpensive parts, about an hour of labor for most vehicles, and the improvement in performance and fuel economy is immediate.

5. Mass Airflow Sensor Failure

The mass airflow (MAF) sensor is positioned between your air filter and the engine's intake manifold. Its job is to measure the precise volume and density of air entering the engine, which the ECU uses to calculate exactly how much fuel to inject. A dirty or failing MAF sensor sends incorrect air volume readings, leading to a rich or lean fuel mixture that causes rough running, hesitation, stalling at idle, poor fuel economy, and increased emissions.

Here in Central Texas, our dusty conditions can clog the MAF sensor's delicate hot-wire element faster than in less arid climates, especially if you regularly drive on gravel roads, unpaved Hill Country tracks, or through construction zones. In many cases, a thorough cleaning with specialized MAF sensor cleaner spray can restore the sensor to full function without replacing it.

Prevention Tip

Replace your engine air filter every 15,000-20,000 miles, or more frequently if you drive in dusty conditions. A clean air filter is the MAF sensor's first line of defense. It's a $15-$25 part that takes two minutes to install and can prevent hundreds of dollars in sensor-related repairs down the road.

What Should You Do When the Light Comes On?

First, don't panic. Second, pay attention to how your car is driving. If it feels normal and the light is steady (not flashing), you can safely drive to our shop for a diagnostic. If the light is flashing, or you notice unusual sounds, vibrations, smoke from the tailpipe, or significant loss of power, pull over safely and give us a call at (480) 969-3311.

When you bring your vehicle to Imports & American Auto Services, we connect our professional-grade diagnostic scanner to read the trouble codes stored in your vehicle's ECU. But here's what separates good diagnostics from lazy diagnostics: the code alone doesn't tell the whole story. A code like P0420 (catalytic converter efficiency below threshold) could be caused by the converter itself, a bad O2 sensor upstream, an exhaust leak, or even worn spark plugs. That's why we always perform a thorough investigation before making any recommendations.

We photograph our findings, send them to your phone, and explain everything in plain English before we do any work. You'll know exactly what's wrong, what it costs to fix, and why it matters — and you'll never be pressured into a repair you don't need.
