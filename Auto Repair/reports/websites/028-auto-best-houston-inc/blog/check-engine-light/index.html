Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

Mike Thompson March 5, 2026 4 min read

You're driving along, everything feels fine, and then it appears: that amber glow on your dashboard. The check engine light. For most drivers, it triggers an immediate wave of anxiety. How serious is it? Can I keep driving? Is this going to cost me thousands?

The truth is that the check engine light covers a vast range of issues — from a five-second fix to a major repair. The light itself is connected to your vehicle's On-Board Diagnostics system (OBD-II), which monitors dozens of systems and sensors throughout the car. When something falls outside of expected parameters, the computer stores a diagnostic trouble code (DTC) and illuminates the light.

Here are the five most common reasons we see at our shop, ranked from least to most serious.

1. Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

This is the single most common reason for a check engine light, and it's also the cheapest to fix. Your gas cap is part of the evaporative emission control system (EVAP), which prevents fuel vapors from escaping into the atmosphere. When the cap is loose, cracked, or missing entirely, the system detects a pressure leak and triggers the code.

What to do: Pull over safely, remove the gas cap, inspect the rubber seal for cracks, and reinstall it with a firm twist until you hear it click. Drive for a day or two — the light should reset on its own. If the seal is visibly damaged, a new cap typically costs under $20.

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your car has between two and four oxygen sensors (O2 sensors) that measure the amount of unburned oxygen in your exhaust. This data helps the engine control module adjust the air-to-fuel ratio for optimal performance and emissions. Over time, sensors degrade from exposure to heat and combustion byproducts.

A failing O2 sensor won't leave you stranded, but ignoring it can reduce fuel economy by up to 40% and eventually damage the catalytic converter — a far more expensive component. Replacement typically runs between $150 and $350 depending on the sensor location and your vehicle make.

3. Catalytic Converter Issues

The catalytic converter transforms harmful exhaust gases (carbon monoxide, hydrocarbons, nitrogen oxides) into less harmful substances. When it begins to fail, you'll usually notice reduced acceleration, a sulfur or rotten-egg smell, and lower fuel efficiency. In many cases, catalytic converter failure is caused by a smaller upstream problem that went unaddressed — like that failing O2 sensor we just mentioned.

A catalytic converter replacement can range from $800 to $2,500+, which is exactly why we recommend addressing check engine lights early. The sensor that costs $200 to replace today could save you $2,000 down the road.

4. Mass Airflow Sensor (MAF) Failure

The MAF sensor measures the volume and density of air entering the engine, which determines how much fuel to inject. When it malfunctions, your car might run rough at idle, hesitate during acceleration, or stall unexpectedly. Common causes include a dirty air filter that lets contaminants reach the sensor, or simple age-related degradation.

Prevention tip: Replace your engine air filter on schedule (every 15,000 to 30,000 miles, depending on driving conditions). A $25 air filter change can prevent a $300+ MAF sensor replacement. Some MAF sensors can also be cleaned with specialized spray, buying you extra time.

5. Spark Plug or Ignition Coil Problems

Spark plugs create the tiny explosion that ignites the fuel-air mixture in each cylinder. Ignition coils supply the high voltage that the spark plugs need to fire. When either component fails, you'll typically experience engine misfires — that unmistakable jerking or stumbling sensation. You might also notice rough idle, reduced power, and higher emissions.

Modern iridium or platinum spark plugs can last 60,000 to 100,000 miles, but they don't last forever. Ignition coils typically match that lifespan. Replacement costs vary: spark plugs are usually $100 to $250 for a set (including labor), while a single ignition coil runs $150 to $300.

Flashing vs. Steady: Know the Difference

A steady check engine light means something needs attention soon, but it's generally safe to continue driving to your destination or to the shop. A flashing check engine light is urgent — it typically indicates an active misfire that's sending unburned fuel into the exhaust system, which can overheat and damage the catalytic converter within minutes. If your light is flashing, reduce speed immediately, avoid heavy acceleration, and get to a shop as quickly as possible.

What to Expect at the Shop

We plug an OBD-II scanner into the port under your dashboard (usually near the steering column) and read the stored trouble codes. These codes point us in the right direction, but they're not the complete answer — they tell us which system has a problem, not necessarily which specific component failed. From there, we perform targeted testing to pinpoint the exact cause before recommending any repairs.
