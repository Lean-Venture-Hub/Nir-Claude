Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

By Mike Thompson March 10, 2026 5 min read

Few things cause more anxiety than watching that amber check engine light flicker to life on your dashboard. Your mind immediately races through worst-case scenarios: a blown head gasket, a dead transmission, a repair bill that rivals your monthly rent. But take a breath. In most cases, the check engine light is alerting you to something manageable — especially if you address it promptly.

At Arturos Garage Auto Mechanics, we diagnose check engine lights every single day. After 20 years of experience and more than 12,000 vehicles serviced, we've identified the five issues that trigger this warning light more often than anything else. Understanding these common causes can help you decide whether to drive straight to the shop or whether a quick fix at home might solve the problem.

1. A Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

This is the most common — and most anticlimactic — reason your check engine light turns on. Your fuel system is sealed, and the gas cap plays a critical role in maintaining that seal. When it's loose, cracked, or missing entirely, your vehicle's evaporative emission system detects a leak and triggers the warning.

Before you do anything else, pull over and check your gas cap. Make sure it clicks when you tighten it. If the cap looks cracked or the rubber seal is deteriorated, replace it. A new gas cap costs around $15 at any auto parts store. After tightening or replacing it, the light should clear on its own within a few driving cycles — typically 50 to 100 miles.

Quick tip: If the light starts flashing instead of staying steady, that indicates a more serious issue like engine misfiring. Pull over safely and call your mechanic — driving with a flashing check engine light can cause catalytic converter damage.

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your vehicle has between two and four oxygen sensors, also called O2 sensors, positioned along the exhaust system. These sensors measure how much unburned oxygen passes through your exhaust and relay that information to the engine control module (ECM). The ECM then adjusts the air-fuel mixture for optimal combustion.

When an O2 sensor fails, your engine can't calibrate the fuel mixture correctly. The result is reduced fuel economy — sometimes by as much as 40% — and increased emissions. You probably won't feel a dramatic difference in how the car drives, which is exactly why people ignore it. But a failing O2 sensor forces your catalytic converter to work harder, and replacing a catalytic converter costs ten to twenty times more than replacing the sensor.

Replacement cost typically runs between $150 and $300 including labor. At our shop, we can diagnose which sensor is failing within 20 minutes using factory-level scan tools that read live data from every sensor in your exhaust system.

3. Catalytic Converter Issues

The catalytic converter is one of the most expensive components in your exhaust system, and it's responsible for converting harmful carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons into less harmful emissions. When it starts to fail, you'll usually notice reduced engine performance, decreased fuel economy, and sometimes a sulfur or rotten-egg smell from the exhaust.

Here's the thing most people don't realize: catalytic converters rarely fail on their own. They're usually damaged by other neglected issues — a bad O2 sensor running the engine too rich, worn spark plugs causing unburned fuel to enter the exhaust, or even an engine oil leak dripping onto the converter. That's why regular maintenance is actually the best way to protect this expensive part.

If your catalytic converter does need replacement, expect to pay between $1,000 and $2,500 depending on your vehicle. At Arturos Garage Auto Mechanics, we always investigate the root cause first. There's no point replacing a catalytic converter if the underlying issue will just destroy the new one too.

4. Worn or Fouled Spark Plugs

Spark plugs ignite the air-fuel mixture in each cylinder, and they wear out over time. Modern iridium or platinum plugs can last 60,000 to 100,000 miles, but conventional copper plugs may need replacement every 30,000 miles. When spark plugs degrade, you might notice rough idling, difficulty starting, engine misfires, or a subtle loss of power during acceleration.

Ignoring worn spark plugs doesn't just affect performance — it creates a chain reaction of damage. Misfiring cylinders send unburned fuel into the exhaust, which overheats the catalytic converter. The O2 sensors read abnormal exhaust levels and continuously adjust the fuel mixture, causing further inefficiency. What starts as a $200 spark plug replacement can snowball into a $2,000 repair if left unaddressed.

Maintenance reminder: Check your owner's manual for the recommended spark plug replacement interval for your specific vehicle. If you can't remember when they were last changed, it's worth having them inspected during your next service visit.

5. Mass Airflow Sensor Failure

The mass airflow (MAF) sensor measures the volume and density of air entering your engine. This data is essential for the ECM to calculate the correct amount of fuel to inject. When the MAF sensor fails or gets contaminated, your engine runs either too rich or too lean, leading to rough idling, stalling, and poor fuel economy.

MAF sensors commonly fail due to contamination — dirt, oil residue, or debris that bypasses a worn air filter. This is another case where a cheap maintenance item (a $20 air filter) can prevent an expensive repair. A new MAF sensor costs between $200 and $400 installed, though sometimes a thorough cleaning with specialized MAF sensor cleaner can restore proper function.

What You Should Do Next

If your check engine light is on, start with the gas cap and see if the light clears within a few days of driving. If it persists, bring your vehicle in for a diagnostic scan.
