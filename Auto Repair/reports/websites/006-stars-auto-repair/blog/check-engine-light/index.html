Why Is My Check Engine Light On? 5 Common Causes

By Mike Thompson March 5, 2026 5 min read

You're driving home from work when that little amber light appears on your dashboard. The check engine light. Your stomach drops, your mind races to worst-case scenarios, and you start mentally calculating repair costs. Before you spiral, take a breath. In most cases, the check engine light signals something manageable — not a catastrophic engine failure.

At STARS Auto Repair, we diagnose check engine lights every single day. After 16 years and thousands of diagnostics, we've found that the vast majority fall into five common categories. Understanding these can help you respond calmly, protect your engine, and avoid getting overcharged at less scrupulous shops.

1. Loose or Damaged Gas Cap

This is the most common — and cheapest — cause of a check engine light. Your gas cap seals the fuel system and maintains pressure in the tank. When it's loose, cracked, or missing entirely, the vehicle's onboard diagnostics detect a fuel vapor leak and trigger the light.

What to do: Pull over safely, tighten or replace your gas cap, and drive for a day or two. If the cap was the issue, the light will turn off on its own. A replacement cap costs around $15 at any auto parts store. If the light persists after a few drive cycles, there may be another issue at play.

2. Failing Oxygen Sensor

Your car has between two and four oxygen sensors that measure unburned oxygen in the exhaust. This data helps the engine computer adjust the fuel-to-air ratio for optimal performance. When a sensor fails, your engine runs less efficiently, burns more fuel, and can eventually damage the catalytic converter.

A failing O2 sensor can reduce your fuel economy by up to 40%. That means a $200–$300 sensor replacement could save you hundreds at the pump over the following months — plus prevent a $1,000+ catalytic converter failure down the road.

Pro Tip

If you notice your gas mileage dropping significantly alongside the check engine light, an oxygen sensor is a likely culprit. Get it diagnosed early to avoid compounding costs.

3. Catalytic Converter Problems

The catalytic converter is part of your exhaust system. It converts harmful carbon monoxide into carbon dioxide before it exits through the tailpipe. When it fails, you'll typically notice reduced performance, decreased fuel economy, and sometimes a rotten egg smell.

Catalytic converter repairs can be expensive — typically $1,000 to $2,500 depending on the vehicle. However, catalytic converters rarely fail on their own. They usually degrade because of neglected upstream issues like failing spark plugs or oxygen sensors. This is why regular maintenance and prompt diagnosis are so important. Fixing a $100 spark plug problem today can prevent a $2,000 catalytic converter problem tomorrow.

4. Spark Plug or Ignition Coil Issues

Spark plugs ignite the air-fuel mixture in your engine's cylinders. Over time, they wear down and misfire — meaning one or more cylinders aren't firing properly. You'll feel this as a rough idle, engine hesitation, sluggish acceleration, or a noticeable vibration.

Signs to watch for:

  • Engine misfires or rough idle
  • Difficulty starting the car, especially in cold weather
  • Noticeable loss of power during acceleration
  • Increased fuel consumption
  • Engine surging or hesitating

Spark plug replacement is one of the most affordable engine repairs — typically $150–$300 for a full set including labor. Most manufacturers recommend replacement every 30,000 to 100,000 miles depending on the type. Ignition coils, which deliver the electrical charge to spark plugs, cost a bit more but are still far cheaper than the engine damage they can cause if ignored.

5. Mass Airflow Sensor Failure

The mass airflow (MAF) sensor measures how much air enters the engine so the computer can deliver the correct amount of fuel. When it fails or gets dirty, the engine runs rich (too much fuel) or lean (too little fuel), causing poor performance, stalling, and increased emissions.

The good news: sometimes the MAF sensor just needs cleaning, not replacing. A can of MAF sensor cleaner costs about $10 and can solve the problem entirely. If cleaning doesn't work, replacement typically runs $200–$400. Either way, ignoring a faulty MAF sensor leads to poor fuel economy and potential engine damage over time.

"The check engine light isn't your car crying wolf. It's your car asking for help before a small problem becomes an expensive one. The smartest thing you can do is get it diagnosed within a week." — Mike Thompson, Owner

When to Take It Seriously — Now

While most check engine light causes are manageable, there are situations that demand immediate attention. If the light is flashing rather than steady, that typically indicates an active misfire that could damage your catalytic converter within minutes. Pull over safely and call for a tow.

Also seek immediate help if you notice any of these alongside the light: steam or smoke from under the hood, unusual engine sounds (knocking, grinding), sudden loss of power, or the temperature gauge reading hot.

The Bottom Line

A check engine light is your car's way of communicating. In most cases, early diagnosis saves money.
